## The Core Idea
Good critique does not just disagree. It pressure-tests universal claims with targeted counterexamples. A strong counterexample is a case where the original rule gives the wrong result.

In philosophy, this is a key transition from beginner to intermediate reasoning: you move from asserting a rule to testing its domain of validity.

## Why This Matters
Universal moral language ("always," "never," "in every case") is easy to state and hard to defend. Edge cases reveal hidden assumptions about context, responsibility, or exceptions.

Counterexamples are not rhetorical tricks. They are diagnostic tools for refining principles.

## Worked Example
Claim: "Lying is always wrong."

Counterexample candidate: lying to a violent aggressor to protect a potential victim.

Now ask:
1. Does the original claim still hold unchanged?
2. If not, what revision preserves the moral insight without collapsing into relativism?

Possible revision:
"Lying is presumptively wrong, but can be justified where truth-telling directly enables grave unjust harm."

## Common Mistake
Using an irrelevant edge case.

A useful counterexample must target the principle directly. If it changes topic or smuggles in unrelated facts, it does not test the original claim.

## Before You Write (Step 2)
Use this template:
1. State one universal rule.
2. Provide one concrete counterexample.
3. Offer a revised, narrower principle.
