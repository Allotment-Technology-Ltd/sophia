## The Core Idea
Condition mistakes create hidden invalidity even when language sounds precise. "Necessary" and "sufficient" are directional tools: confusing direction often breaks an argument while leaving fluent prose.

If P is sufficient for Q, then P guarantees Q. If P is necessary for Q, then Q cannot occur without P. Keeping direction straight is essential in policy and ethics arguments.

## Why This Matters
Condition confusion produces classic errors:
- affirming the consequent,
- denying the antecedent,
- overstating policy guarantees.

In advanced writing, markers look for whether your inferential direction matches your claim strength.

## Worked Example
"Being a square is sufficient for being a rectangle."

Correct inference:
Square -> Rectangle.

Invalid reversal:
Rectangle -> Square.

In argument form:
"This is a rectangle, therefore it is a square" fails because sufficiency was reversed into necessity.

## Common Mistake
Using "requires" and "ensures" interchangeably.

"Requires" often signals necessity.
"Ensures" signals sufficiency.
When these are swapped, the argument drifts into invalidity.

## Before You Write (Step 2)
Build two condition statements from a real issue:
1. one necessary condition,
2. one sufficient condition.

Then test each with an "if...then..." sentence and one non-example.
