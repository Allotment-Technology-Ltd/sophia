## The Core Idea
A valid argument can still have false premises. Validity is about form, not truth-value. In this lesson, your first task is to map structure clearly: which sentences are premises, and which sentence is the conclusion?

Many novice essays fail because the conclusion is emotionally plausible but not logically supported by stated premises. Your job is to separate "sounds right" from "follows."

## Why This Matters
If you cannot map argument structure, critique becomes vague and personal. Once structure is clear, critique becomes precise:
- Is premise 2 doing the real work?
- Is a hidden premise required?
- Does the conclusion overreach?

## Worked Example
Argument:
"Suffering is bad. Policy X reduces suffering. Therefore policy X is morally preferable."

Structure:
1. Premise: Suffering is bad.
2. Premise: Policy X reduces suffering.
3. Conclusion: Policy X is morally preferable.

Now critique can focus productively on premise quality (for example, whether X causes hidden harms), rather than attacking style.

## Common Mistake
Mistaking a background assumption for a conclusion.

If your conclusion is only implied but never stated, readers cannot evaluate whether your premises really support what you want to claim.

## Before You Write (Step 2)
Draft with labels:
- P1:
- P2:
- P3 (if needed):
- Therefore C:

Then ask: "Could someone accept all premises but reject C?" If yes, the inference needs repair.
