## The Core Idea
Ethics asks a different question from social science. Social science asks what people do. Ethics asks what people ought to do. Good philosophical writing keeps those two levels separate before trying to connect them.

When learners struggle in early ethics, the issue is often not "bad opinions." The issue is structural confusion: descriptive observations are treated as if they already contain moral force. This lesson trains you to spot that gap.

## Why This Matters
If you skip the is/ought distinction, your argument sounds confident but has no valid bridge. You need at least one normative premise that explains why the descriptive fact has moral relevance.

Without that bridge, readers can respond: "You told me what happens. You did not yet show what should happen."

## Worked Example
Claim: "People often break promises under pressure" (descriptive) does not by itself establish "therefore promises need not be kept" (normative).

To make this an argument, add a bridge premise:
"If a norm is frequently broken under pressure, that norm loses its binding force."

Now readers can challenge the bridge directly, which is exactly what good philosophy encourages.

## Common Mistake
Jumping from prevalence to justification:
"Everyone does X, so X is acceptable."

This confuses sociological frequency with moral legitimacy.

## Before You Write (Step 2)
Use this mini-structure:
1. One descriptive claim.
2. One explicit normative bridge premise.
3. One conclusion that follows from both.
