## The Core Idea
Explanations answer "why this happened." Arguments answer "why you should accept this claim." The same sentence can function as either, depending on context and purpose.

In philosophy writing, this distinction prevents category errors. If your reader asks for justification and you provide only causal background, your paragraph sounds informative but fails argumentatively.

## Why This Matters
Many beginner essays mix explanatory and argumentative moves without signaling the shift. As a result, claims appear unsupported even when the writer has useful material.

Train this diagnostic question:
"Is this sentence trying to *prove* something, or trying to *make sense* of something already accepted?"

## Worked Contrast
Sentence: "The policy failed because implementation was rushed."

As explanation:
The failure is accepted; sentence gives cause.

As argument:
If used to support "therefore the policy should be redesigned," then you still need a normative bridge from causal diagnosis to policy recommendation.

## Common Mistake
Treating causal language ("because") as proof by default.

"Because" can introduce reasons, causes, or assumptions. You must identify which role it plays in your paragraph.

## Before You Write (Step 2)
Write two short statements on one topic:
1. one explanation of a known fact;
2. one argument for a contested claim.

Label each explicitly so the difference is visible.
