## The Core Idea
Ethical disagreement often reflects different standards of justification, not only different facts. Deontology asks whether an action respects duty, rights, and constraints. Consequentialism asks whether outcomes are overall better.

At degree level, students are expected to compare these frameworks charitably, not caricature one side as "cold rules" and the other as "anything goes."

## Why This Matters
When you identify framework conflict, you can diagnose *why* people disagree, not just *that* they disagree.

In policy writing, this helps you produce stronger arguments:
- deontological check: does this policy violate a constraint?
- consequentialist check: what are aggregate harms and benefits?

## Worked Contrast
Case: harming one innocent person to save five.

Typical deontological concern:
The person is being used merely as a means.

Typical consequentialist concern:
Refusing intervention may produce greater total harm.

A better essay does two things:
1. states each side in its strongest form;
2. explains which costs it is willing to prioritize and why.

## Common Mistake
Switching standards mid-paragraph.

For example, claiming "rights are absolute" and then justifying exceptions only by better outcomes, without explaining the shift.

## Before You Write (Step 2)
Write in three moves:
1. Deontological verdict + reason.
2. Consequentialist verdict + reason.
3. Your synthesis: which standard governs this case, and under what limits?
